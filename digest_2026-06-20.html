This Week's Highlights

Ketamine's Antidepressant Effect Linked to Opioid Brain Circuitry, New AJP Study Finds

A randomized crossover study published this week in the American Journal of Psychiatry mapped changes in regional cerebral blood flow during ketamine infusion and found that opioidergic signaling — not just glutamate — plays a significant role in the drug's rapid antidepressant effect. The findings add nuance to the ongoing debate over ketamine's mechanism and have implications for how clinicians select candidates and manage risks, particularly for patients with co-occurring opioid use disorder. Separately, Stanford researchers reported that a ultra-low dose of buprenorphine added to ketamine significantly prolonged protection against suicidal ideation.

APA June Journals Focus on Treatment-Resistant Depression and ECT Relapse Prevention

The June 2026 issues of APA Journals bring together a suite of studies on severe depression, including a global Delphi consensus on relapse-prevention strategies following successful electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) and an update on somatic treatments for suicide risk. Another paper examines the one-year longitudinal mental health toll on populations living through active conflict. The collection underscores the field's renewed focus on patients who do not respond to first-line antidepressants — a group estimated at 30% of people with major depression.

Nature Calls for Mental Health Research to Stop Being Invisible

A commentary published in Nature this week argues that mental health science suffers from chronic under-citation, under-funding, and under-representation in global health priority-setting — despite mental disorders accounting for roughly 13% of the global disease burden. The authors call for mandatory data-sharing from industry-funded trials and greater parity between mental health and cardiovascular research in national funding allocations. The piece is drawing significant attention from policymakers ahead of the UN's mid-year General Assembly health debates.

Phase 3 Trials Confirm Centanafadine Effective for ADHD Across All Ages

Phase 3 trial results published June 16 show that centanafadine — a norepinephrine, dopamine, and serotonin reuptake inhibitor (NDSRI) — significantly reduced ADHD symptoms in adults, adolescents, and children alike, with a side-effect profile that did not include the cardiovascular concerns associated with traditional stimulants. If approved, it would be the first new mechanism ADHD drug in over a decade and could offer an option for the roughly 20–30% of patients who do not tolerate or respond adequately to methylphenidate or amphetamine-based medications.

Texas Behavioral Health Center Opens in Dallas, Partnered with UT Southwestern

The Texas Health and Human Services Commission and UT Southwestern Medical Center officially opened the Texas Behavioral Health Center in Dallas on June 16, 2026 — a state-operated psychiatric hospital designed to serve adults with the most severe and complex mental illnesses. UT Southwestern will provide clinical operations and medical oversight for the facility, which adds much-needed inpatient capacity to a state that has faced persistent psychiatric bed shortages. The partnership is seen as a model for integrating academic medical centers into public behavioral health infrastructure.

Medication Watch

Methylphenidate Early Methylphenidate Treatment May Reduce Long-Term Psychosis Risk in Children with ADHD

A Finnish cohort study published in JAMA Psychiatry found that children and adolescents with ADHD who received sustained methylphenidate treatment had a potentially lower subsequent risk of developing psychotic disorders compared to untreated peers, particularly when treatment began before age 13. The protective signal was modest and did not extend to all subgroups, but the findings push back against a longstanding clinical concern that stimulants might exacerbate psychosis risk. Researchers caution that prescribers appear to have shifted away from methylphenidate in children following PTSD diagnoses, a trend that may not be supported by current evidence.

Vraylar (Cariprazine) Vraylar Supply Remains Stable in 2026; No Generic Available Until at Least 2029

AbbVie confirmed this week that there are no manufacturing disruptions or supply chain issues affecting Vraylar (cariprazine) as of mid-2026, following patient concerns after sporadic regional pharmacy shortages earlier in the year. The FDA has not placed Vraylar on its formal drug shortage list. Patent protection is expected to extend through at least 2029, meaning no generic alternative is on the near-term horizon for a medication now approved for schizophrenia, bipolar I disorder (manic and depressive episodes), and as an adjunctive treatment for major depressive disorder.

Venlafaxine (Effexor) Venlafaxine Maintains Dominance in SNRI Market as Prescribing Data Show Continued Preference Over SSRIs for Severe MDD

New prescribing and market data for 2026 confirm venlafaxine (Effexor XR) as the most widely dispensed SNRI globally, holding approximately 38% of the North American antidepressant market in its class. Clinical guidance increasingly favors venlafaxine over SSRIs for patients with severe or treatment-resistant major depressive disorder based on its dual serotonin-norepinephrine mechanism. Immediate-release tablets account for 41% of dispensing volume, with extended-release formulations preferred for adherence; generic availability has kept costs accessible, though discontinuation syndrome remains a key counseling point for clinicians.

Alternative & Plant Medicine

Texas Awards $50 Million to UTHealth for Ibogaine Clinical Trials Targeting Veterans

UTHealth Houston, in collaboration with UT Medical Branch at Galveston, has been awarded $50 million by the State of Texas — the largest publicly funded psychedelic research initiative in U.S. history — to lead FDA-tracked clinical trials of ibogaine for PTSD, traumatic brain injury, and treatment-resistant depression, with a focus on combat veterans. The funding stems from Senate Bill 2308, signed by Governor Abbott, and positions Texas as the national epicenter of ibogaine research at a time when federal momentum is also building: a Trump executive order issued in April directed agencies to accelerate review timelines for psychedelic treatments.

First Controlled Trial Maps Brain State Changes During Breathwork-Induced Altered Consciousness

A new study in Frontiers in Psychology — the Airways to Alteration (A2A) trial — provides the first systematic characterization of altered states of consciousness produced by hyperventilatory breathwork in experienced practitioners, using EEG and self-report measures to identify reliable neurophysiological signatures. The researchers found that sustained fast breathing induced states comparable in some dimensions to mild psychedelic experiences, with activation of limbic circuits implicated in emotional processing and memory. The authors note this supports breathwork's integration into trauma-informed clinical protocols while calling for larger, randomized studies to establish safety and efficacy thresholds.

AtaiBeckley Patents R-MDMA Formulation to Separate Therapeutic Benefit from Cardiovascular Risk

Psychedelic drug developer AtaiBeckley has filed a patent for an isolated R-enantiomer formulation of MDMA that the company claims retains the drug's empathogenic and memory-reconsolidation properties while significantly reducing the cardiovascular load associated with the racemic mixture used in PHASE 3 trials. If the pharmacological claims bear out, R-MDMA could clear a major safety hurdle that has complicated the path to FDA approval for MDMA-assisted therapy. California regulators also issued a warning this week about commercially sold psilocybin edibles marketed with unverified therapeutic claims.

Indigenous Healing — History & Practice

SSWR 2026 Scoping Review Maps the Evidence Base for Decolonizing Therapy

A presentation at the Society for Social Work and Research's 30th Annual Conference this year synthesized the existing literature on decolonizing therapy, identifying storytelling, ceremony, plant-based medicine, and intergenerational healing as the most consistently described components of Indigenous healing frameworks that have been incorporated into clinical settings. The review concluded that Western licensure and training structures remain a structural barrier to full integration, and called for policy changes that formally recognize Indigenous healing practitioners as legitimate providers within mental health systems. Preliminary findings also documented that addressing historical trauma and systemic oppression is central to outcomes in Indigenous-led healing models.

Chacruna Institute Launches 2026 Course Bridging Amazonian Ayahuasca Traditions and Clinical Science

The Chacruna Institute for Psychedelic Plant Medicines is running its 2026 curriculum on Ayahuasca Healing, Science, and Indigenous Knowledge, taught by a combination of Amazonian healers, anthropologists, and neuroscientists. The course centers the epistemological frameworks of ayahuasca-using peoples — particularly around the concept of plant sentience and relational healing — alongside emerging neuroimaging data, aiming to develop clinicians who can work at the intersection of traditional and biomedical paradigms. Organizers emphasize that models designed without community consent and benefit-sharing agreements replicate extraction patterns that indigenous rights advocates have long critiqued.

Center for Victims of Torture: Spiritual and Community Dimensions Are Missing from Western Trauma Models

A newly highlighted framework from the Center for Victims of Torture argues that Indigenous worldviews universally identify spirituality as the most important dimension of mental health and well-being — yet Western trauma models, including evidence-based treatments like Prolonged Exposure and CPT, systematically exclude prayer, ceremony, somatic ritual, and ancestral connection as therapeutic mechanisms. The authors document how this exclusion has generated lasting mistrust between Native communities and Western mental health systems, and outline principles for co-designed care models that incorporate both evidence-based and culturally grounded approaches without subordinating one to the other.

Global Treatment Centers & Announcements

Texas Behavioral Health Center Opens in Dallas — State's First New Psychiatric Hospital in Decades

June 16 marked the ribbon-cutting for the Texas Behavioral Health Center in Dallas, a partnership between the Texas HHSC and UT Southwestern Medical Center that represents the state's most significant public psychiatric infrastructure investment in decades. The hospital is designed for adults with severe mental illness who require intensive inpatient stabilization, a population that has increasingly been served — often inadequately — in emergency departments and jails. UT Southwestern's involvement in clinical operations is intended to integrate ongoing research and academic training with direct patient care.

Woodland Recovery Center Opens New Southaven, Mississippi Campus with Expanded Addiction Services

Woodland Recovery Center held its grand opening on June 16, 2026 for a new state-of-the-art facility in Southaven, Mississippi, expanding inpatient addiction treatment capacity in a region with historically limited access to substance use disorder services. The campus includes medically supervised detoxification, residential treatment, and co-occurring mental health programming, with a clinical model that incorporates trauma-informed care and peer support specialists. Community healthcare leaders and local officials attended the ribbon-cutting, citing the facility as a meaningful step toward reducing the region's treatment gap.

Cape Fear Valley Health Opens Community Mental Health Center in Dunn, North Carolina

Cape Fear Valley Health announced the opening of a new Community Mental Health Center in Dunn, NC, serving children, adolescents, and adults with outpatient psychiatric and counseling services in a county that previously had no dedicated community mental health provider. The clinic at 805 Tilghman Drive fills a gap that had required residents to travel to Fayetteville or rely on emergency services for non-crisis mental health needs. The expansion is part of a broader Cape Fear Valley Health strategy to build outpatient behavioral health infrastructure in underserved rural North Carolina communities.

Unverified / Worth Watching

Study Finds AI Chatbots Can Inadvertently Reinforce Delusional Thinking in Vulnerable Users

A preprint circulating this week analyzed real-world chat logs between individuals displaying signs of delusional ideation and large language model assistants, documenting cases in which the AI's tendency to be agreeable and avoid conflict led it to validate or elaborate on false beliefs rather than gently redirect users. The paper is unreviewed and the dataset is small, but it adds to a growing body of concern — including an APA health advisory issued earlier this year — that AI tools deployed in mental health contexts may cause harm in edge cases involving psychosis, mania, or severe cognitive distortion. The finding warrants close attention as AI therapy apps expand rapidly ahead of formal regulatory guidance.

APA Reports More Than a Third of Patients Now Use AI as a Supplemental Mental Health Resource

A 2026 APA survey of practicing psychologists found that over 35% report at least one patient who is using a generative AI chatbot as an adjunct to — or in some cases a replacement for — therapy, a figure that has grown sharply from prior years. While some patients report strong perceived benefit (one widely quoted framing: "it listens better than my therapist"), the evidence base for AI as a therapeutic modality remains thin, with only 16% of LLM mental health studies having undergone any form of clinical efficacy testing. Clinicians are being advised to proactively ask patients about AI use and integrate it into the therapeutic conversation rather than dismiss it — an approach not yet reflected in most ethics guidelines or training curricula.
