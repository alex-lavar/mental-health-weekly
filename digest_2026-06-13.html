This Week's Highlights

Memphis Opens First Walk-In Youth Mental Health Center — No Appointment, No Wait

A new children's wellness center in Memphis began accepting walk-in visits on June 1, allowing parents to bring children in crisis to a licensed clinician at any hour without scheduling ahead. The Alliance Foundation raised $45 million for the combined adult and children's campus, with the State of Tennessee contributing $12 million between the two facilities. The no-appointment model is being watched nationally as a potential template for reducing the gap between crisis and care in underserved communities.

Compass Pathways Accelerates Psilocybin Launch Plan After Positive FDA Dialogue

Following a Johns Hopkins–led roadmap published in April 2026, Compass Pathways announced an accelerated commercial launch plan for COMP360 — its synthetic psilocybin formulation for treatment-resistant depression — after "positive discussions" with the FDA about the path to approval. The company expects a potential regulatory decision before year's end, which would make COMP360 the first FDA-approved psilocybin therapy in the United States. Clinicians at Hopkins and partner sites are also finalizing a training curriculum to ensure therapist readiness if approval proceeds.

Men's Mental Health Month 2026: "Partners in Care" Addresses Long-Standing Treatment Gaps

June is Men's Mental Health Month, and this year's theme — "Partners in Care: Advancing Men's Health Through Connection, Education, & Advocacy" — marks a deliberate pivot from individual willpower messaging toward structural and relational support frameworks. Research consistently shows men access mental health services at roughly half the rate of women, with stigma, provider access, and financial barriers cited as primary obstacles. Advocates are calling for expanded community-based models and employer-driven mental health benefits as key levers for closing the gap.

AI Behavioral Health Tools Expanding Rapidly Ahead of Clinical Validation, Report Finds

A new Becker's Behavioral Health report documents a sharp increase in the use of AI-driven mental health apps, chatbots, and digital triage tools across health systems — driven largely by workforce shortages and rising demand. While some platforms show promising early outcomes data, the report warns that most have not undergone the kind of randomized clinical testing required of conventional treatments, and that commercial deployment is outpacing regulatory scrutiny. Researchers urge payers and systems to demand validated evidence before scaling AI-based mental health tools.

Medication Watch

Methylphenidate Psychosis Risk and Methylphenidate: Age of Treatment Onset Is the Critical Variable

A reanalysis of a large-scale cohort study clarifies a nuanced finding: methylphenidate is not associated with elevated psychosis risk in children with ADHD overall, but its protective effect is specific to those who begin treatment before age 13. Children diagnosed and treated later in adolescence did not show the same benefit, pointing to a developmental window during which stimulant treatment may shape long-term psychiatric trajectories. The findings reinforce the importance of timely ADHD diagnosis rather than watchful waiting in younger children.

Vraylar / Cariprazine Vraylar in 2026: Supply Constraints and What Patients and Prescribers Need to Know

A MedFinder supply update highlights that while Vraylar (cariprazine) is not on the FDA's formal drug shortage list, patients in some regions have encountered fulfillment delays at retail pharmacies in 2026, particularly for lower-dose strengths. AbbVie attributes the constraints to manufacturing scheduling rather than a sustained shortage, but clinicians are advising patients to maintain extra lead time on refills and to contact their prescribers early if supply runs out. No reformulation or discontinuation is anticipated.

Venlafaxine / Effexor UK Pharmacists Authorized to Substitute Venlafaxine Formulations Amid Recurring Supply Disruptions

Recurring supply disruptions in the UK have prompted updated official guidance allowing pharmacists to substitute between venlafaxine formulations — immediate-release, extended-release capsule, and extended-release tablet — without requiring a new prescription, in order to prevent treatment gaps. Clinicians emphasize that abrupt discontinuation of venlafaxine carries significant risk given its short half-life of three to seven hours, making supply continuity a patient safety issue rather than a mere inconvenience. Patients are advised to contact prescribers early if they anticipate a refill delay rather than stopping cold.

Alternative & Plant Medicine

VA Launches MDMA-Assisted Mental Health Therapy Trial for Veterans

The U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs has formally launched a clinical trial of MDMA-assisted therapy for veterans with treatment-resistant PTSD, marking the VA's first foray into psychedelic-assisted treatment in its own clinical infrastructure. The trial follows years of pressure from veteran advocacy groups and builds on Phase 2 data showing significant PTSD symptom reductions in civilian populations. Researchers will track response rates, safety outcomes, and protocol adherence to build the evidence base needed for broader VA integration if results are positive.

$50M Federal Ibogaine Mandate: Agencies Begin Implementing Trump's April Executive Order

Federal agencies are now operationalizing the April 2026 executive order directing $50 million toward ibogaine research for veterans, with the FDA instructed to fast-track a review and the Department of Defense tasked with supporting clinical trials. The order also expands Right to Try pathways so eligible veterans can access ibogaine in licensed hospital-grade international settings while domestic approval is pursued. A 2024 Stanford study showing 88% average PTSD symptom reduction in special operations veterans — combined with neuroimaging evidence of increased cortical thickness post-treatment — underpins the policy momentum.

Sayulita Wellness Launches Dedicated Ibogaine Platform Focused on Medical Safety Standards

Sayulita Wellness Retreat in Mexico has launched a dedicated ibogaine treatment platform explicitly designed to prioritize clinical safety over retreat aesthetics, including mandatory cardiac screening, on-site physician oversight, and structured integration support following each session. The move reflects growing demand for medical-grade ibogaine access outside the U.S., where the substance remains Schedule I, and comes as scrutiny of informal retreat settings intensifies following safety incidents at unmonitored facilities. The platform joins Europe's Ambio Life Sciences in positioning physician-led ibogaine programs as the responsible standard of care.

Indigenous Healing — History & Practice

Dr. Jennifer Mullan's Decolonizing Therapy Framework Gains Traction in Clinical Training

Dr. Jennifer Mullan's Decolonizing Therapy® practice — which centers anti-oppressive, trauma-informed care rooted in community accountability rather than individual pathology — is increasingly being integrated into graduate-level clinical training programs and continuing education for licensed therapists. The framework challenges DSM-centric diagnostics as products of colonialism and calls on practitioners to examine how systemic power structures manifest as psychological distress in marginalized communities. Growing interest reflects a broader field-wide reckoning with whose cultural assumptions are embedded in standard therapeutic models.

Native Mental Health 2026: Urban Indigenous Wellness Programs Expand Access to Traditional Healing

Native-led wellness programs in urban centers are expanding in 2026, combining traditional healing modalities — ceremony, storytelling, plant medicine, and land-based practices — with licensed mental health services to serve Indigenous people who have been historically underserved by or excluded from Western clinical systems. Programs like the Urban Native Collective's Pathways to Wellness in Kansas City received expanded funding through a 2025 grant cycle and now offer free therapy, fitness, and community-based healing without insurance requirements. Researchers at affiliated institutions are working to build a peer-reviewed evidence base that respects Indigenous data sovereignty and community control over research outcomes.

Global Treatment Centers & Announcements

Hackensack Meridian Carrier Clinic Opens $40M Child and Adolescent Mental Health Expansion

Carrier Clinic in Belle Mead, New Jersey opened a $40 million, 43,000-square-foot expansion on June 8, adding 52 inpatient beds and lowering the minimum treatment age from 12 to 7 — directly addressing a critical gap in psychiatric care for younger children. The new facility includes multi-sensory treatment rooms, an academic teaching center, and recreational spaces, all housed within the Steven & Alexandra Cohen Foundation-funded Cohen Community Resource & Education Center. The opening is the most significant expansion of youth inpatient psychiatric capacity in the New Jersey–New York region in recent years.

Newsom Announces New Behavioral Health Projects to Expand Mental Health Services Across California

California Governor Gavin Newsom announced a wave of new behavioral health infrastructure projects in May 2026, leveraging Proposition 1 bond funds to expand community-based psychiatric services, crisis stabilization units, and supportive housing for people with serious mental illness across multiple counties. The projects are designed to address persistent gaps in outpatient and transitional care that have contributed to visible mental health crises in major California cities. Full site development timelines extend through 2028, with county-level implementation plans required as a condition of state funding.

Wisconsin Lawmakers Approve $10M for Western Region Mental Health and Treatment Facility

The Wisconsin legislature approved $10 million in state funding for a new mental health and substance use treatment facility serving western Wisconsin, a region that has faced among the state's highest rates of untreated mental illness and opioid-related emergency visits. The facility will offer integrated dual-diagnosis treatment — addressing co-occurring substance use and psychiatric conditions simultaneously rather than sequentially — and is expected to break ground later in 2026. Local health advocates called the approval a long-overdue investment in a part of the state that has been chronically underfunded for behavioral health services.

Unverified / Worth Watching

Ibogaine for OCD: Clinics Report Dramatic Results, but Controlled Evidence Is Absent

Several ibogaine retreat operators and clinics are promoting the treatment as transformative for obsessive-compulsive disorder, citing anecdotal patient reports of rapid, lasting symptom relief. While ibogaine's mechanism — involving serotonin reuptake inhibition and neuroplasticity-promoting 5-HT2A activity — provides a plausible theoretical basis for OCD benefit, there are currently no published controlled trials supporting this indication. Clinicians urge caution: the risk profile of ibogaine, including cardiac arrhythmia risk, is well-documented, and expanded use for unvalidated conditions without safety data is concerning.

Leading Depression Centers Issue Cautions on Premature Psilocybin Integration Into Clinical Care

A consensus statement from the U.S. National Network of Depression Centers' Task Group on Psychedelics, published in eClinicalMedicine (Lancet), urges a "balanced approach that prioritizes rigorous scientific standards while considering the urgency of the need for better treatment options" — a direct pushback against the commercial enthusiasm surrounding psilocybin. The group warns that existing evidence, while promising, does not yet meet the bar needed for routine clinical use outside of research settings, and that training standards, adverse event monitoring, and equity frameworks must be built before wide deployment. The statement is worth watching as it may influence payer decisions and health system policies even ahead of FDA action.

Thought Field Therapy Resurfaces Online — Proponents Claim Efficacy, Evidence Remains Weak

Thought Field Therapy (TFT), a tapping-based technique that claims to rebalance "thought fields" through sequences of body tapping, has seen a resurgence in online wellness communities as practitioners market it for anxiety, trauma, and phobias. Academic reviews note that most supporting evidence comes from uncontrolled case reports, and a key 2003 experiment found no difference between prescribed TFT tapping sequences and randomly selected ones — undermining the core theoretical claim. TFT falls in a category of approaches that feel experiential benefit for some users but lack the controlled evidence needed to be considered evidence-based; those attracted to it should weigh that distinction carefully.
