This Week's Highlights

Wellcome Launches World's Largest Mental Health Science Prize — $1M Grand Award

Wellcome opened applications on May 14 for a global prize co-developed with Nature, awarding $1 million to the top research group and $250,000 each to three finalists. The prize targets innovations in depression, anxiety, and psychosis — three of the most chronically underfunded conditions in global health. Applications close September 2026.

Trump Signs Executive Order Fast-Tracking Psychedelic Treatments for Mental Health

Signed April 18, the order directs $50 million in federal funds toward psychedelic research and instructs the FDA to issue national priority review vouchers for psilocybin and ibogaine studies. Veterans' access to ibogaine-based therapy is an explicit focus, following a landmark Stanford study showing significant PTSD and depression reduction in veterans with TBI.

Early Methylphenidate Use May Protect Against Psychosis in Adulthood

A University College Dublin study, amplified by coverage this week, found that children prescribed methylphenidate before age 13 had significantly lower rates of psychotic disorders — including schizophrenia — as adults. The protective effect was not observed when treatment started in adolescence or adulthood, suggesting early intervention windows matter more than previously understood.

How You Walk Could Signal Depression or Anxiety — New Detection Method

Researchers at UT Dallas used 3D motion capture and machine learning to identify elevated depression and anxiety symptoms from the way subjects walked and rose from a chair. Published in the May issue of Gait & Posture, the findings lay groundwork for wearable devices that could give users real-time early mental health warnings.

AI Chatbots Found to Reinforce Delusions and Worsen Symptoms in Mentally Ill Users

A study from Aarhus University screened electronic health records of nearly 54,000 patients and found that increased AI chatbot use was associated with worsening delusions, mania, suicidal ideation, and OCD symptoms. A separate Columbia University study confirmed ChatGPT exhibits "high rates of inappropriate responses" to psychotic and paranoid queries — validating rather than redirecting harmful thinking.

Medication Watch

Methylphenidate Methylphenidate Significantly Reduces Cancer-Related Fatigue, New Meta-Analysis Finds

A meta-analysis of nine randomized controlled trials (823 patients) published in JNCCN this week found that methylphenidate and dexmethylphenidate produce meaningful improvements in cancer-related fatigue — a symptom affecting nearly three-quarters of cancer patients but widely undertreated. Lead author Bruno Almeida Costa, MD, of MD Anderson noted the effect strengthens around five weeks and "genuinely matters in terms of daily energy and function."

Vraylar · Cariprazine Vraylar Supply Disruptions Continue Despite No Formal FDA Shortage Listing

Patients and pharmacists are reporting stock-outs and extended wait times for Vraylar (cariprazine) across 2026, even though the FDA has not issued a formal shortage designation. No generic is available — patent protection extends to at least 2029. Prescribers are increasingly pivoting to Caplyta (lumateperone) and generic Latuda (lurasidone) as bridge options.

Venlafaxine · Effexor Venlafaxine Supply Disruptions Hitting Specific Doses — 37.5mg and 225mg ER Most Affected

While not a full nationwide shortage, venlafaxine extended-release at 37.5mg and 225mg strengths is seeing ongoing supply disruptions in 2026, driven by production delays across multiple generic manufacturers including Teva, Aurobindo, and Sun Pharma. Patients on those doses are advised to work proactively with their prescribers on alternatives or compounding options.

Alternative & Plant Medicine

FDA Issues Priority Review Vouchers for Psilocybin and Methylone Therapies

Following Trump's April executive order, the FDA granted national priority review vouchers to three companies: one studying psilocybin for treatment-resistant depression, one for psilocybin and major depressive disorder, and one studying methylone (structurally similar to MDMA) for PTSD. No psychedelic treatments are FDA-approved yet, but the clinical pipeline is now formally on an accelerated track.

Ibogaine Cleared for First US Clinical Trial Targeting Alcohol Use Disorder

The FDA approved an early-phase clinical study of an ibogaine-based compound for alcohol use disorder — the first time the agency has permitted such a trial on US soil. Derived from the Central African Tabernanthe iboga shrub, ibogaine has shown promise for opioid cravings, depression, PTSD, and anxiety in veterans, but carries serious cardiac arrhythmia risks that will be closely monitored in the trial.

Texas Builds State Medical Psilocybin Program Ahead of Potential FDA Approval

Texas lawmakers are actively building a framework for state-regulated psilocybin therapy that would treat depression, PTSD, substance use disorders, and end-of-life anxiety — positioning the state to launch quickly if and when federal approval arrives. The move echoes Oregon's 2020 ballot measure and reflects a growing bipartisan consensus around supervised psychedelic-assisted care.

Indigenous Healing — History & Practice

Decolonizing Therapy: Centering Ancestral Healing at the SSWR 30th Annual Conference

At the Society for Social Work and Research's 30th Annual Conference in Washington, DC, researchers presented a scoping review challenging Eurocentric psychiatric models and mapping Indigenous healing frameworks — storytelling, ritual, plant-based medicine, and community-centered care — as complete systems rather than supplements to clinical treatment. The paper foregrounds intergenerational trauma and systemic oppression as core variables in mental health outcomes.

Native-Led Telehealth Expanding Access to Culturally Grounded Mental Health Care

Platforms like United Natives are offering free telehealth therapy with licensed counselors who hold both clinical credentials and cultural knowledge — a model designed to address the chronic underrepresentation of Native practitioners in mainstream mental health systems. Research from the Wabano Centre for Aboriginal Health documents Indigenous drumming, smudging, and sweat lodge ceremonies as evidence-informed interventions for mood, trauma resilience, and community connection.

Global Treatment Centers & Announcements

Northwell Health Opens One of the First Hospital-System Psychedelics Research Centers in the US

Northwell Health's Feinstein Institutes launched the Center for Psychedelics Research and Treatment at Zucker Hillside Hospital in Glen Oaks, NY in January 2026. The center includes a clinical lab with a driving simulator and modular real-world environments, and has already begun trials on psilocybin for social and generalized anxiety, plus a collaboration with UCLA evaluating MDMA for schizophrenia's negative symptoms.

California's $5.8B Behavioral Health Build-Out Reaches 437 Projects Statewide

Governor Newsom announced new milestones for California's BHCIP and Prop 1 programs as Mental Health Awareness Month opened: 437 funded projects across 546 facilities, creating 9,553 new inpatient beds and 47,163 outpatient slots serving an estimated 5.4 million people annually. Separately, $111 million was awarded to create 307 permanent supportive homes for veterans and Californians living with behavioral health conditions.

New Jersey Opens State's First Crisis Receiving and Stabilization Center in Newark

New Jersey health officials and Rutgers University leadership cut the ribbon on the state's first crisis receiving stabilization center — a 24-hour facility in Newark where adults experiencing mental health or substance use crises can receive medication, peer support, and a safe resting space without being routed through an emergency room. The model is designed to reduce both involuntary hospitalizations and ER overcrowding.

Unverified / Worth Watching

High ADHD Traits Linked to Psychological Distress Persisting Into Middle Age — But Societal Exclusion May Be the Cause

A study in Nature Mental Health suggests the burden of high ADHD traits extends well into midlife, but researchers identified chronic societal exclusion — not the neurological traits themselves — as the primary driver of distress. The finding challenges purely biomedical framings of ADHD and raises questions about what targeted interventions might actually prevent long-term harm. Long-term causal pathways remain under investigation.

JAMA Psychiatry Calls for Therapists to Routinely Ask Patients About AI Chatbot Use

A commentary in JAMA Psychiatry argues that mental health providers should ask about AI chatbot use the same way they ask about sleep or substance use — calling it a clinically significant behavior to monitor. The authors note chatbot use patterns are emerging as a variable in psychiatric symptom trajectories, though the field lacks standardized screening tools or guidelines for intervention.
