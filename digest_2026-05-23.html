This Week's Highlights

Nearly 1.2 Billion People Now Living with Mental Disorders, Landmark Lancet Study Finds

A sweeping new analysis published in The Lancet on May 21 found that 1.2 billion people — roughly 14% of the global population — were living with a mental disorder in 2023, representing a 95.5% increase since 1990. Anxiety and depression drove the largest share of cases and continued to rise well past their COVID-era peaks, signaling a persistent structural shift rather than a pandemic blip. Researchers analyzed 12 disorder categories across 204 countries, making this one of the most comprehensive global prevalence datasets ever assembled.

One Dose of Psilocybin Changes the Human Brain — and the Effect Predicts Better Well-Being a Month Later

A new neuroimaging study found that a single high dose of psilocybin measurably increased brain entropy — a marker of neural flexibility — within one hour, as recorded by EEG. Crucially, the magnitude of this entropy boost predicted greater psychological insight and improved well-being up to a month after the session. The findings add mechanistic weight to psilocybin's therapeutic promise and may help researchers identify who is most likely to benefit from psychedelic-assisted therapy.

Wellcome Launches $1 Million Global Prize to Accelerate New Mental Health Interventions

Wellcome, one of the world's largest science-funding organizations, announced a new global prize in May 2026 aimed at research teams developing novel treatments for depression, anxiety, and psychosis. The top winner will receive $1 million USD, with three additional finalists each awarded $250,000. The prize is designed to celebrate and amplify early-stage innovations that could reach underserved populations, particularly in low- and middle-income countries where treatment gaps are widest.

Louisiana Legislature Moves to Fund Clinical Trials for Ibogaine, MDMA, and Other Psychedelics

Louisiana's Senate Bill 43, advancing through the legislature this week, would create a state health initiative to fund clinical research into ibogaine, MDMA, psilocybin, and related compounds for mental health and substance use disorders. The move follows President Trump's April executive order directing federal agencies to fast-track psychedelic research, and makes Louisiana one of the first states to formally back such trials with public dollars. Advocates say the legislation could be especially impactful for veterans and first responders with treatment-resistant PTSD.

FDA Sets May 31 Decision Deadline for CTx-1301, a Novel Once-Daily Dexmethylphenidate Formulation

The FDA has accepted Cingulate Inc.'s New Drug Application for CTx-1301, a new extended-release formulation of dexmethylphenidate designed to provide smoother, longer-lasting ADHD symptom coverage with a lower abuse potential profile than existing products. The PDUFA target action date is May 31, 2026, meaning a decision is imminent. If approved, CTx-1301 would be the first new stimulant mechanism approved for ADHD in several years, entering a market still grappling with extended-release methylphenidate shortages.

Medication Watch

Methylphenidate Childhood Methylphenidate Treatment May Protect Against Later Psychosis, Large Finnish Study Finds

A landmark cohort study published in JAMA Psychiatry and led by researchers at University College Dublin analyzed nearly 700,000 individuals born in Finland between 1987 and 1997. Among children diagnosed with ADHD before age 13, sustained methylphenidate treatment was associated with a meaningfully lower long-term risk of developing psychotic disorders, including schizophrenia. The findings suggest the medication may do more than manage childhood symptoms — it may alter developmental neurobiology in ways that reduce vulnerability to serious mental illness decades later.

Vraylar (Cariprazine) Vraylar Availability Squeeze Intensifies Despite No Formal FDA Shortage Listing

Patients and prescribers report growing difficulty obtaining Vraylar (cariprazine) at retail pharmacies, even though the FDA has not declared a formal drug shortage. Analysts attribute the gap to a surge in prescriptions following Vraylar's 2022 approval for adjunctive major depressive disorder, combined with pharmacy ordering systems that understock lower-volume specialty drugs. With no generic available until at least 2029 and prior-authorization hurdles at most insurers, patients with treatment-resistant depression and bipolar disorder are facing meaningful access barriers.

Venlafaxine (Effexor) Venlafaxine Supply Tightens for Key Doses; Phase 3 GAD Trial in Japan Meets All Endpoints

Certain strengths of venlafaxine extended-release — particularly 37.5 mg and 225 mg capsules — are experiencing intermittent shortages at US pharmacies in 2026, consistent with broader stimulant and SNRI availability pressures. On the research front, a Viatris Phase 3 study in Japanese adults with generalized anxiety disorder met its primary endpoint (Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale improvement versus placebo) and all seven secondary endpoints, potentially supporting regulatory filings in Japan and informing global dosing guidelines. The global venlafaxine market is currently valued at $3.62 billion and growing at 4.5% annually.

Alternative & Plant Medicine

Trump Signs Executive Order to Fast-Track Psychedelic Drug Reviews, Directs $50M in Federal Matching Funds

President Trump signed "Accelerating Medical Treatments for Serious Mental Illness" on April 18, 2026, directing the FDA to expedite review of psychedelics with Breakthrough Therapy designation and instructing HHS to deploy $50 million to match state investments in psychedelic research. The order also carves out a Right-to-Try pathway for patients to access investigational psychedelic compounds — including ibogaine — that have cleared basic safety requirements while still under full FDA review. Joe Rogan, who attended the signing, has been publicly credited by Trump for sparking the administration's interest in ibogaine for PTSD and traumatic brain injury.

Pilot Trial: Psilocybin With Psychotherapeutic Support Shows Durable Benefits in Treatment-Resistant Depression

A newly published pilot clinical trial evaluated a two-session psilocybin protocol (25 mg each) bookended by three preparatory and six integration psychotherapy sessions for patients with treatment-resistant depression. Participants showed significant reductions in depression severity maintained through follow-up, with the psychotherapy scaffolding credited for helping patients contextualize and sustain their experiences. The study adds to a growing body of evidence shaping FDA deliberations, with psilocybin-assisted therapy for depression considered a plausible approval candidate in the near term.

Breathwork for Chronic Stress: Meta-Analysis Finds Medium-to-Large Effect Sizes Across 40 Randomized Trials

A systematic review and meta-analysis of 40 randomized controlled trials on respiratory interventions for anxiety and stress reported medium to large improvements in anxiety symptoms — including dimensional anxiety and panic — compared to inactive controls. Somatic breathwork techniques, which pair conscious breathing with body-awareness exercises, are gaining particular traction in clinical settings for trauma histories, chronic stress, and nervous system dysregulation. Researchers note that choosing the right technique matters: diaphragmatic and slow-paced breathing outperformed hyperventilation-based protocols for most anxiety presentations.

Indigenous Healing — History & Practice

SSWR 2026: Decolonizing Therapy — Centering Indigenous and Ancestral Healing in Mental Health Practices

Presented at the Society for Social Work and Research's 30th Annual Conference, this paper examines how mainstream mental health systems have historically marginalized Indigenous, Latinx, and non-Western healing frameworks — and proposes concrete pathways for integrating them. Core elements include Indigenous epistemologies and storytelling, ritual and plant-based medicine, intergenerational trauma frameworks, and community-centered (rather than individually focused) models of wellness. The authors argue that licensing constraints, institutional resistance, and lack of dedicated funding remain the largest structural barriers to genuine integration.

Native-Led Wellness Initiatives Expand Urban Access to Indigenous Mental Health Models

Programs like Pathways to Wellness — run through the Urban Native Collective in Kansas City — are scaling their model of combining free therapy, fitness, and community-based healing specifically designed for Native peoples living in urban settings. A 2025 funding expansion through the Pathways to Wellness Fund is allowing the initiative to reach more individuals cut off from reservation-based traditional practices. Advocates emphasize that land connection, ceremony, and collective healing structures are not supplemental to care but foundational to mental health for many Indigenous communities.

Indigenous Spirituality as Resilience: How Ceremony and Ancestral Practice Counteract Historical Trauma

A body of research and practitioner literature reviewed this week highlights the role of ceremonial practice, ancestral spiritual connection, and communal ritual in buffering the psychological effects of historical trauma among Indigenous populations. Scholars argue that Western clinical models, which frame spirituality as secondary to evidence-based intervention, inadvertently replicate colonial dynamics by pathologizing culturally normative healing behaviors. Integrating Indigenous epistemologies — on their own terms rather than as adjuncts to Western therapy — is increasingly framed as both a clinical and an ethical imperative.

Global Treatment Centers & Announcements

Amae Health and Novant Health Open Specialized Outpatient Clinic for Complex Mental Illness in Charlotte, NC

Amae Health, which focuses on individuals with severe and complex mental health conditions often underserved by standard outpatient care, opened its first North Carolina clinic on May 18 in partnership with Novant Health at 301 S. McDowell Street, Suite 320, Charlotte. The integrated care model co-locates psychiatric, primary care, and social support services, targeting patients with diagnoses like treatment-resistant depression, schizoaffective disorder, and complex trauma. The partnership represents a growing trend of health systems contracting with specialty mental health operators to address gaps in serious mental illness care.

New Mexico Certifies Five New Community Behavioral Health Clinics in 2026

New Mexico's Health Care Authority is adding five new Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics across six counties in 2026, expanding a model that provides comprehensive outpatient mental health and substance use disorder services regardless of a patient's ability to pay. CCBHCs are federally certified to offer 24/7 crisis services, peer support, and case management under a prospective payment system, making them more financially sustainable than traditional outpatient models. New Mexico's expansion reflects a national trend, with over a dozen states scaling CCBHC networks this year.

NYC Launches "Elevate You" Youth Mental Health Clinics at Two Public Hospital Sites

The Mamdani administration announced new Elevate You clinics at NYC Health + Hospitals/Woodhull and Queens, providing adolescents and young adults with integrated behavioral health, primary care, reproductive care, and vocational support services under one roof. The $4 million in seed funding from MetroPlusHealth is designed to reach young New Yorkers who have historically fallen through gaps between pediatric and adult mental health systems. The clinics are part of a broader city strategy to increase youth mental health capacity following post-pandemic demand spikes that strained school and hospital resources.

Unverified / Worth Watching

9D Breathwork: Combining Holotropic Breathing, Binaural Beats, and Neuroscience — Claims Ahead of the Evidence

"9D Breathwork" is a commercially marketed modality that layers holotropic breathing techniques with binaural beats, guided visualization, and somatic cues, claiming to produce rapid emotional breakthroughs and trauma release. Proponents point to the established science behind breathwork's effect on the autonomic nervous system and individual testimonials of profound experiences. However, no peer-reviewed clinical trials specifically evaluate 9D Breathwork as a distinct protocol — the brand's unique claims remain unsupported by controlled evidence, and the technique requires careful facilitation to avoid adverse effects such as hypocapnia-induced tetany.

First FDA-Allowed Clinical Trial of Noribogaine for Alcohol Use Disorder — A Milestone Worth Watching

In a significant regulatory first, the FDA has allowed an early-phase clinical study of noribogaine hydrochloride — a metabolite derivative of ibogaine — to proceed as a potential treatment for alcohol use disorder. This marks the first time the FDA has permitted a clinical trial of any ibogaine-derived compound since research was effectively halted in the U.S. in the 1990s due to cardiac arrhythmia concerns. Noribogaine is believed to retain ibogaine's anti-addictive properties with a potentially safer cardiovascular profile, but this remains to be established in the trial. Results are expected to inform FDA's posture on the broader ibogaine compound class.

Serotonin May Worsen Tinnitus — New Evidence Challenges Assumptions About SSRI Safety

Emerging research suggests that serotonin — the neurotransmitter whose reuptake is blocked by SSRIs, one of the most commonly prescribed drug classes in psychiatry — may actually exacerbate tinnitus rather than relieve it. If confirmed in larger studies, the finding would have significant implications for the tens of millions of patients who take SSRIs and report tinnitus as a side effect, a symptom currently classified as rare and idiosyncratic. Researchers caution that this evidence is preliminary and does not yet support changing prescribing practices, but the mechanism is considered biologically plausible and deserves controlled investigation.
