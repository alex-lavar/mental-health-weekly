This Week's Highlights

Wellcome Launches World's Largest Prize for Mental Health Science — $1 Million for Breakthrough Interventions

The Wellcome Prize for Mental Health Science, developed in partnership with Nature, opened applications this week with a top award of USD $1 million and three finalist prizes of $250,000 each. Eligible submissions must demonstrate scientific rigor and measurable impact on depression, anxiety, or psychosis — and must be informed by lived experience. The application window runs through September 18, 2026, with the winner announced in June 2027.

Compass Pathways COMP360 Achieves Primary Endpoint in Second Phase 3 Trial for Treatment-Resistant Depression

Compass Pathways announced that its COMP006 Phase 3 trial of COMP360 psilocybin met its primary endpoint with high statistical significance (P<0.001), producing a clinically meaningful 3.8-point reduction in depression severity. The result replicates findings from the earlier COMP001 trial and clears a major milestone on the path toward an FDA New Drug Application. Topline data were also presented as breaking poster data at the 2026 ASCP Annual Meeting.

Serotonin May Worsen Tinnitus, Challenging Core Assumption About Antidepressants

A study from Oregon Health & Science University and Anhui University, published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, found that elevated serotonin levels amplify tinnitus-like symptoms in mouse models by activating a direct serotonin-to-auditory-circuit pathway. The finding may explain why a subset of patients report worsening ringing in the ears on SSRIs and SNRIs. Researchers caution the work is preclinical and urge patients not to discontinue medication without consulting a provider.

White House Executive Order Expands "Right to Try" Pathways for Novel Mental Health Treatments

President Trump signed the Accelerating Medical Treatments for Serious Mental Illness executive order in April, directing the FDA to issue National Priority Vouchers and widen Right to Try access for treatment-resistant psychiatric conditions. The order has drawn particular interest from researchers working on ibogaine, psilocybin, and other investigational compounds that remain Schedule I at the federal level. Implementation guidance from the FDA is expected later this year.

University of Huddersfield Launches Major Research Centre to Address Mental Health Inequalities Across West Yorkshire

Timed to Mental Health Awareness Month, the University of Huddersfield officially established a new dedicated mental health research centre focused on reducing disparities in care across West Yorkshire — a region with some of the UK's highest rates of mental health need. The centre will embed community-based participatory research methods and prioritize underserved populations including young people and ethnic minority communities.

Medication Watch

Methylphenidate FDA PDUFA Decision Due May 31 on CTx-1301, a Next-Generation Dexmethylphenidate

Cingulate's CTx-1301 — a proprietary dexmethylphenidate formulation engineered to eliminate midday rebound and multiple-daily dosing requirements — reaches its FDA Prescription Drug User Fee Act target action date tomorrow, May 31, 2026. If approved, it would represent the first new methylphenidate-class product approved in several years and could address ongoing adherence challenges with existing ER formulations that remain on backorder.

Vraylar · Cariprazine Patients Reporting Growing Pharmacy Stock-Outs for Vraylar Despite No Formal FDA Shortage

AbbVie reports no manufacturing disruptions, and Vraylar (cariprazine) has not been added to the FDA's official drug shortage list — yet patients and pharmacists across multiple states describe increasing difficulty filling prescriptions on time. No generic cariprazine is expected before patent expiry in 2029 or later. Patients experiencing supply gaps are advised to contact their prescriber about therapeutic alternatives or manufacturer patient assistance programs.

Venlafaxine · Effexor New Serotonin-Tinnitus Research Raises Questions for Venlafaxine and SNRI Users

This week's OHSU/PNAS serotonin-tinnitus findings are particularly relevant for venlafaxine (Effexor) users: as an SNRI, venlafaxine raises both serotonin and norepinephrine, and it is one of the most widely prescribed antidepressants in the US. Tinnitus is already listed among its known side effects. Prescribers are advising patients who notice worsening tinnitus to discuss dose adjustments, but to avoid abrupt discontinuation given venlafaxine's well-documented withdrawal effects.

Alternative & Plant Medicine

Magnesium-Ibogaine Formulation Shows Accelerated Recovery in Veterans with TBI and PTSD — npj Mental Health Research

A study published in Nature npj Mental Health Research characterized the subjective healing experiences of U.S. Special Operations veterans treated with a magnesium-ibogaine protocol. Researchers identified four recurrent domains: guided autobiographical trauma re-appraisal, altered-self and mystical connectedness, emotional resolution with surges of forgiveness and renewed purpose, and an "embodied healing" sense of neural repair accompanied by cognitive clarity. The magnesium component is designed to reduce the cardiac risks historically associated with ibogaine.

Ibogaine Draws Renewed Federal Attention as Addiction Treatment — CNN Explainer

CNN published a comprehensive explainer on ibogaine's growing policy profile, noting that the Trump administration's Right to Try expansion and the VA's increasing openness to psychedelic research have given the Schedule I compound more mainstream institutional visibility than at any point in its history. The piece reviews the evidence base — strongest for opioid use disorder and PTSD — alongside the cardiac safety concerns that have complicated broader clinical adoption.

Indigenous Healing — History & Practice

Native Communities Advance Indigenous Wellness Framework as Urban Programs Expand Funding in 2026

Native American mental health advocates are scaling the "Indigenous Wellness Framework" — a community-centered model integrating ceremony, storytelling, plant-based medicine, and intergenerational trauma work — in both reservation and urban contexts. The Urban Native Collective's Pathways to Wellness program received expanded 2026 funding to bring culturally specific services to cities where Native people often lack access to tribally run health systems. Practitioners continue to highlight licensing and institutional barriers that exclude traditional healers from clinical settings.

SSWR 30th Annual Conference Features Paper on Decolonizing Therapy, Centering Ancestral Healing Practices

The Society for Social Work and Research's 2026 anniversary conference included a formal academic presentation titled "Decolonizing Therapy: Centering Indigenous and Ancestral Healing in Mental Health Practices." The paper argues that Western psychological constructs often pathologize culturally normative expressions of distress and calls for systemic changes to supervision, licensure, and insurance reimbursement that would legitimize Indigenous approaches alongside evidence-based clinical models.

Global Treatment Centers & Announcements

California's Prop 1 Build-Out Hits Milestone: 9,553 New Beds and 47,163 Outpatient Slots Awarded Statewide

Governor Newsom's May 5 announcement confirmed that California's Behavioral Health Continuum Infrastructure Program has now awarded $5.8 billion across 546 facilities, exceeding Proposition 1 statewide goals in just two years. The expansion is projected to serve more than 5.4 million Californians annually. The state also credited the effort with helping achieve the first reduction in unsheltered homelessness in 15 years — a 9% drop. New groundbreakings this week include a 12-bed acute psychiatric hospital in San Diego County.

New Jersey Opens State's First Crisis Receiving Stabilization Center at University Hospital Newark

New Jersey's inaugural crisis receiving and stabilization center held its ribbon-cutting April 1 on the University Hospital campus in Newark. The center provides up to 24-hour stays for adults experiencing acute mental health or substance use crises, offering medication management, peer specialist support, and a clinical rest environment — a lower-acuity, less costly alternative to emergency department admission. The model is part of a national push to redirect psychiatric crises away from ERs.

Unverified / Worth Watching

Single Dose of Psilocybin Linked to Rapid Reduction in Chronic Suicidal Ideation — Small Open-Label Pilot

A pilot study published in the Journal of Clinical Psychiatry found that a single dose of psilocybin was associated with rapid, robust, and durable reductions in suicidal ideation. The result is striking given how refractory chronic suicidal ideation can be, but the open-label design, small sample, and lack of a control arm mean the finding needs replication in a randomized trial before clinical conclusions can be drawn. Worth watching as a signal.

The "90-Day Neuroplasticity Window" After Ibogaine Is Becoming a Dominant Recovery Framework — But Peer Review Is Thin

A growing number of ibogaine clinics and integration therapists are promoting the idea that the 90 days following an ibogaine session represent a uniquely open neuroplasticity window during which somatic work, EMDR, and breathwork yield outsized results. The concept resonates with broader post-psychedelic integration literature, but no peer-reviewed studies have directly tested the 90-day framing or quantified the plasticity timescale. Patients considering ibogaine should ask providers to distinguish between established safety protocols and marketing-adjacent concepts like this one.
