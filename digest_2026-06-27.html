This Week's Highlights

The FDA Put Psilocybin and an MDMA-Like Drug on a 1-to-2 Month Approval Track

Following President Trump's April executive order accelerating psychedelic drug research, the FDA issued National Priority Vouchers to Compass Pathways (synthetic psilocybin for treatment-resistant depression), the Usona Institute (psilocybin for MDD), and Transcend Therapeutics (methylone for PTSD). These vouchers compress standard 10–12 month application reviews down to one or two months — a historic milestone that could make COMP360 the first classic psychedelic ever approved by the U.S. government before year's end.

Huge Genetics Study Reveals Hidden Links Between 14 Psychiatric Conditions

An international study led by the Psychiatric Genomics Consortium analyzed genetic data from more than six million people and found that mental health disorders cluster into five broad genetic groups — each driven by a distinct set of inherited variants. The findings explain why conditions like depression, anxiety, ADHD, and schizophrenia so often co-occur, and challenge the rigid diagnostic boundaries that have structured psychiatric treatment for decades. Published in Nature, the study is the most comprehensive mapping of shared psychiatric genetics to date.

ADHD Stimulant Medications Work Differently Than Previously Thought

Researchers at Washington University in St. Louis have found that stimulant medications like methylphenidate and amphetamines don't work primarily by raising overall dopamine levels, as long assumed, but by fine-tuning dopamine signaling in specific neural circuits. The discovery opens a path toward more targeted ADHD therapies with fewer side effects, and may also explain why the medications have inconsistent efficacy across patients.

Mental Health Research Is Too Often Invisible — It Is Time to Change That

A commentary in Nature argues that mental health interventions have historically been shaped more by social control than therapeutic intent — and that this legacy continues to distort funding priorities and research agendas today. The authors call for systematic reforms to surface underrepresented voices in mental health science, particularly from low- and middle-income countries, and to disclose conflicts of interest in psychiatric literature more rigorously.

VA Launches MDMA-Assisted Mental Health Therapy Trial for Veterans

The U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs has opened a new clinical trial to evaluate MDMA-assisted therapy for veterans with severe PTSD and alcohol use disorder, backed by over $23 million in external funding alongside 19 other active VA psychedelic trials. The launch follows the executive order on accelerating treatments for serious mental illness, signaling a meaningful federal shift toward studying therapies that were taboo just five years ago.

Medication Watch

Methylphenidate Long-Term Methylphenidate Use Raises Brain Dopamine Transporter Density by 24%

A study from Brookhaven National Laboratory found that adults with ADHD who took methylphenidate for 12 months showed a 24% increase in dopamine transporter density in key brain regions — a level significantly higher than in unmedicated adults without ADHD. Researchers are evaluating whether this neuroadaptation over time may reduce medication efficacy, potentially explaining why some patients require dose escalations after years of treatment.

Vraylar · Cariprazine Vraylar's Pediatric Indications Expand — Now Approved for Schizophrenia (13+) and Bipolar I (10+)

AbbVie's Vraylar (cariprazine) has extended its reach with FDA approvals for adolescent schizophrenia (ages 13 and up) and manic/mixed episodes of bipolar I disorder (ages 10 and up), adding to its existing adult approvals for MDD as adjunctive therapy and bipolar depression. The pediatric expansions, backed by over 20 clinical trials involving more than 8,000 patients, make cariprazine one of the most broadly indicated atypical antipsychotics currently available.

Venlafaxine · Effexor FDA Evaluates Venlafaxine Safety Signal: False-Positive Radioisotope Test Results

The FDA has been evaluating a potential safety signal involving venlafaxine (Effexor) and false-positive radioisotope imaging results, possibly stemming from a drug interaction affecting test accuracy. Clinicians ordering nuclear medicine scans for patients on venlafaxine are advised to disclose the medication to their imaging team and review recent FDA communications. The agency has not yet issued a formal label update, but the signal is under active monitoring.

Alternative & Plant Medicine

Ibogaine Therapeutic Potential and Cardiac Safety: A 2026 Scoping Review

A comprehensive scoping review published in PMC examines ibogaine's promise for opioid use disorder, methamphetamine addiction, and PTSD alongside its most significant clinical concern: cardiac arrhythmia risk, particularly QT prolongation. The authors find that physician-supervised settings with pre-treatment cardiac screening substantially reduce adverse events, and point to Texas's $50 million research allocation and ongoing FDA Phase 1/2 trials as markers of a rapidly maturing clinical infrastructure around this formerly fringe compound.

Breathwork for Chronic Stress: Does the Technique Matter? A New Systematic Review

A peer-reviewed systematic review in MDPI's Healthcare journal examined the relative efficacy of different breathwork approaches for chronic stress and mental health, finding medium-to-large improvements in anxiety across all controlled breathing modalities compared to passive controls. Notably, slower diaphragmatic breathing and cyclic sighing outperformed hyperventilatory techniques for sustained anxiety relief, giving clinicians more evidence-based guidance on which protocols to recommend. The authors call for standardized outcome measures to better compare techniques across future trials.

2026 Psychedelics & Healing Initiative: From Fringe to Clinical Mainstream

The Global Wellness Institute's annual trends report highlights the convergence of psychedelic science with traditional plant medicine in 2026 — tracking the rise of legal psilocybin retreats in Oregon and Colorado, growing demand for ketamine-assisted therapy, and the emerging integration of somatic practices alongside psychedelic sessions. The report notes that practitioner certification programs have proliferated, but standards remain inconsistent, with calls for a unified credentialing framework gaining traction in the field.

Indigenous Healing — History & Practice

Decolonizing Mental Health Through Traditional Healing Frameworks: Insights from Canada, China, Singapore, and the U.S.

A new cross-national study published in PubMed compares how traditional healing frameworks — including Indigenous Canadian ceremony, Traditional Chinese Medicine, Singapore's Malay-Islamic healing practices, and Native American healing circles — can be integrated into contemporary mental health services. All four traditions emphasize communal healing rituals, ancestral connectivity, and embodied knowing as foundational to wellness, contrasting sharply with the Western individualist and symptom-reduction model. The authors argue that true integration requires institutional reform, not just cultural competency training layered onto existing systems.

Decolonizing Therapy: Centering Indigenous and Ancestral Healing in Mental Health Practices

A paper presented at the Society for Social Work and Research's 30th Annual Conference describes a scoping review identifying core elements of decolonizing therapy: incorporation of Indigenous epistemologies (storytelling, ritual, plant medicine), a focus on intergenerational trauma, community-centered interventions that prioritize collective well-being over individual pathology, and critical interrogation of Western diagnostic constructs that may mislabel culturally normative behaviors as disorders. The authors highlight persistent implementation barriers — licensing constraints, institutional resistance, and funding gaps — as the primary bottleneck preventing wider adoption.

Journey to Self-Determination in Indigenous Cognitive Health Research in Canada

A new paper from Canadian researchers outlines a community-led model for Indigenous cognitive health studies that centers community sovereignty over data, research questions, and interpretation. The model, grounded in the "Visiting Way" methodology, emphasizes reciprocal relationships and authentic dialogue between researchers and communities — explicitly rejecting extractive research practices that have historically defined Indigenous health studies. The authors position community self-determination not as an ethical add-on but as the structural prerequisite for any valid Indigenous health research.

Global Treatment Centers & Announcements

Texas Behavioral Health Center Opens in Dallas — UT Southwestern & State Partnership

The Texas Health and Human Services Commission and UT Southwestern Medical Center held a ribbon-cutting ceremony this month for the new Texas Behavioral Health Center in Dallas, a state-of-the-art facility designed to integrate comprehensive psychiatric treatment, training across behavioral health disciplines, and new care models aimed at improving outcomes for patients and families. UT Southwestern will operate the facility and use it as a clinical training ground for the next generation of mental health professionals across Texas.

Hackensack Meridian Health Opens $40M Child & Adolescent Psychiatric Facility in New Jersey

Carrier Clinic's new 43,000-square-foot expansion — part of the Hackensack Meridian Health network — opened this month with 52 inpatient beds for children as young as seven, lowering the previous admission threshold from age 12. The $40 million facility includes multi-sensory treatment rooms, The Cohen Community Resource & Education Center, and an academic teaching suite to expand psychiatric training capacity in a state facing acute shortages of youth mental health beds.

New Youth Mental Health Center Opened June 1 in Memphis

A new youth-focused mental health center opened its doors on June 1 in Memphis, Tennessee, adding much-needed community-based outpatient capacity for children and adolescents in a region historically underserved by psychiatric infrastructure. The center offers individual therapy, group programming, and family support services, with a focus on bridging the gap between inpatient discharge and long-term community care.

Unverified / Worth Watching

GLP-1 Drugs (Ozempic-Class) May Curb Addiction by Targeting Brain Reward Circuits

Early-stage research suggests that GLP-1 receptor agonists — the same class of drugs behind weight-loss medications like semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) — may reduce cravings for alcohol, opioids, and other substances by modulating dopamine pathways in the brain's reward system. Several clinical trials are underway, and anecdotal reports from patients taking GLP-1 drugs for metabolic conditions have sparked wide interest. No rigorous RCT evidence exists yet for addiction indications; clinicians urge caution before adoption outside of metabolic contexts.

Vagus Nerve Stimulation Showing Sustained Relief in Treatment-Resistant Depression

Vagus nerve stimulation (VNS), already FDA-cleared for epilepsy and a limited depression indication, is generating renewed interest after several observational studies reported sustained antidepressant effects over 12–24 months in patients who had failed multiple medication trials. Researchers believe VNS may work by modulating limbic and prefrontal circuits over time rather than delivering acute symptom relief. The evidence base remains limited and largely non-randomized; independent replication in rigorous controlled trials is still needed before broader clinical adoption is warranted.

AI Therapeutic Chatbots Enter Formal Clinical Trials — Efficacy and Ethics Under Scrutiny

Several AI-powered mental health chatbots are now entering formal randomized controlled trials after years of consumer deployment with minimal clinical evidence. Proponents argue that AI therapists can dramatically extend access to evidence-based CBT for underserved populations; critics point to documented risks of chatbots reinforcing suicidal ideation and to the absence of peer-reviewed outcome data for most commercial products currently on the market. Regulatory frameworks remain underdeveloped; watch this space closely over the next 12–18 months.
